**Question:**  
A school theater sold a total of 150 tickets for a play. Adult tickets cost \$12 each and student tickets cost \$8 each. The total amount of money collected from ticket sales was \$1,560. How many adult tickets were sold?

A) 45  
B) 60  
C) 75  
D) 90  

**Correct Answer:** D  

**Explanation:**  

1. **Set up the system of equations**  
   Let  
   - \(a\) = number of adult tickets  
   - \(s\) = number of student tickets  

   From the problem we have  
   \[
   \begin{cases}
   a + s = 150 &\text{(total tickets)}\\[2mm]
   12a + 8s = 1560 &\text{(total revenue)}
   \end{cases}
   \]

2. **Eliminate one variable**  
   Multiply the first equation by 8 so the coefficients of \(s\) match:  
   \[
   8a + 8s = 1200
   \]

   Subtract this new equation from the revenue equation:  
   \[
   (12a + 8s) - (8a + 8s) = 1560 - 1200
   \]
   \[
   4a = 360
   \]

3. **Solve for the remaining variable**  
   \[
   a = \frac{360}{4} = 90
   \]

4. **Check (optional but recommended)**  
   If \(a = 90\), then \(s = 150 - 90 = 60\).  
   Revenue check: \(12(90) + 8(60) = 1080 + 480 = 1560\), which matches the given total.

**Why D is correct:**  
The calculations show that 90 adult tickets were sold, which corresponds to option **D**.

**Common misconceptions addressed:**  

- **Mixing up coefficients:** Some students might subtract the equations incorrectly, e.g., \(12a - 8a = 4a\) is correct, but they might mistakenly subtract the constant terms the wrong way, leading to \(a = 45\) (option A).  
- **Dividing by the wrong number:** After obtaining \(4a = 360\), dividing by 2 instead of 4 would give \(a = 180\), an impossible answer because it exceeds the total tickets.  
- **Using the wrong equation for elimination:** If a student eliminates \(a\) instead of \(s\) without careful arithmetic, they might obtain \(s = 75\) (option C), which does not satisfy both original equations.  

By following the systematic elimination method and checking the result, the correct answer is clearly **D) 90**.

**Question:**  
A delivery robot that weighs 50 kg and a delivery truck that weighs 5,000 kg are each pushed forward by identical electric motors that provide a constant horizontal force of 200 N. Ignoring air resistance and rolling friction, which vehicle will have the greater acceleration and why?

**A)** The robot will accelerate more because its smaller mass means a larger acceleration for the same force.  
**B)** The truck will accelerate more because its larger mass allows it to convert the force into more motion.  
**C)** Both will accelerate at the same rate because the applied force is identical for the two vehicles.  
**D)** Neither will accelerate because the force is too small to overcome the vehicles’ inertia.

**Correct Answer:** A  

**Explanation:**  
Newton’s second law states that the net force on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration ( F = m·a ). Solving for acceleration gives a = F/m. With the same force (200 N) applied, the vehicle with the smaller mass (the robot, 50 kg) will have a larger acceleration: a_robot = 200 N / 50 kg = 4 m/s², while a_truck = 200 N / 5,000 kg = 0.04 m/s².  

**Why the other choices are wrong:**  
- **B** reflects the misconception that a heavier object “gets more motion” from the same force; in fact, the larger mass resists acceleration.  
- **C** ignores the role of mass; equal forces do not guarantee equal accelerations.  
- **D** misinterprets “inertia” as a threshold that must be overcome before any motion occurs. Inertia is simply the resistance to change in motion, quantified by mass, and any non‑zero net force will produce some acceleration, however small.  

Thus, the robot’s lower mass results in a greater acceleration, illustrating Newton’s second law in a real‑world context.

**Question:**  
A designer places a corner of a rectangular table at point \(P(1,2)\) on a coordinate grid.  
First, the entire floor‑plan is rotated \(90^{\circ}\) counter‑clockwise about the origin.  
After the rotation, the plan is slid (translated) 5 units to the right and 2 units up.  
What are the coordinates of the corner that was originally at \(P\)?

**A)** \((3,\,3)\)  
**B)** \((5,\,4)\)  
**C)** \((-1,\,7)\)  
**D)** \((4,\,-1)\)

**Correct Answer:** A

**Explanation:**  

1. **Rotate \(P(1,2)\) \(90^{\circ}\) CCW about the origin**  
   - A \(90^{\circ}\) counter‑clockwise rotation sends \((x, y)\) to \((-y, x)\).  
   - \((-y, x) = (-2, 1)\).  
   - After rotation the point is at \((-2, 1)\).

2. **Translate the rotated point 5 units right and 2 units up**  
   - Translation adds \((+5, +2)\) to the coordinates.  
   - \((-2 + 5,\; 1 + 2) = (3, 3)\).

3. **Result**  
   - The corner that started at \((1,2)\) ends at \((3,3)\).

**Why the other choices are wrong**

- **B) \((5,4)\):** This result comes from forgetting the sign change in the rotation (using \((y, x)\) instead of \((-y, x)\)).  
- **C) \((-1,7)\):** This adds the translation before rotating, which reverses the order of operations.  
- **D) \((4,-1)\):** This applies the translation only in the horizontal direction and uses the wrong vertical shift.

The correct sequence—rotate first, then translate—gives \((3,3)\).

**Question:**  
A midsized city located near a large lake has noticed that over the past decade its summers have become noticeably hotter. Scientists explain that the higher temperatures cause more water to evaporate from the lake and from soils. However, the city’s water managers have also observed that summer rainfall has not increased; in fact, the area experiences longer dry spells. Which statement best explains how the hotter summers are affecting the local water cycle and the city’s water supply?

**A)** The extra evaporation creates more clouds directly over the city, so the region should receive more rain and have a larger water supply.  
**B)** Higher temperatures increase evaporation, adding more moisture to the atmosphere, but changing wind and weather patterns can move that moisture away, leading to less local precipitation and reduced water availability.  
**C)** Warmer air holds less water, so the increased temperature actually reduces evaporation, causing drought conditions.  
**D)** The temperature rise only influences ocean currents; it does not affect the evaporation‑condensation‑precipitation cycle over land and lakes.

**Correct Answer:** B  

**Explanation:**  
- **Why B is correct:** Hotter air can hold more water vapor, so evaporation from the lake and soils does increase. Climate‑change research shows that while evaporation rises, the resulting atmospheric moisture does not always fall as rain in the same place; altered wind patterns and larger‑scale circulation can transport the moisture elsewhere. Consequently, the city may experience longer dry periods despite higher evaporation, reducing the local water supply for drinking, irrigation, and hydroelectric power.  
- **Why A is a common misconception:** More evaporation does add moisture to the atmosphere, but it does not guarantee that clouds and rain will form directly over the same area. Cloud formation depends on many factors, including atmospheric stability and wind direction.  
- **Why C is incorrect:** Warm air actually *holds* more water vapor, not less. Therefore, higher temperatures increase—not decrease—evaporation.  
- **Why D is incorrect:** Temperature changes affect the entire water cycle, including evaporation from lakes and soils, condensation into clouds, and precipitation. The influence is not limited to ocean currents.  

Understanding this cause‑and‑effect relationship helps students see how a seemingly simple change (hotter summer) can ripple through the water cycle and impact real‑world water resources.
